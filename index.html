1. Not an Alphabet, But a Syllabary

To understand this language, we have to realize that Mycenaean writing, known as Linear B, wasn't like the writing we use today. It was a highly specialized tool used almost exclusively by palace scribes for administrative records‚Äîcounting sheep, jars of oil, and weapons.

Phonetics

Unlike the modern alphabet where one letter equals one sound, Linear B was a syllabary. Each symbol represented a syllable (like da, de, di, do, du).

The Sound

It sounded much more archaic. For example, it had a "Q" sound (labiovelar). "King" in Classical Greek is Basileus, but in Mycenaean, it was QA-SI-RE-U.

The Flaw

The script was clumsy and didn't fit the Greek language well. That is why only a few elite scribes knew how to use it.

2. The Million Dollar Question

How did they say "I Love You"?
This is tricky because Linear B was used for accounting, not romance. We have lists of chariots, but no love letters. However, linguists have reconstructed the words based on the roots found in the tablets.

3. The Collapse (1200 BC)

Around 1200 BC, the Mycenaean civilization fell apart due to a "perfect storm" of catastrophes:

  • The "Sea Peoples": Mysterious naval raiders who burned cities to the ground.
  • The Dorian Invasion: A theory suggesting Greeks from the north overthrew the elite.
  • System Failure: The centralized economy collapsed when trade routes were cut.
  • Natural Disasters: Evidence of massive earthquakes and droughts.

4. The Aftermath: The Greek Dark Age

When the palaces collapsed, the paperwork system (Linear B) vanished. The general population never learned to read or write it, so the script died with the scribes. This started the Greek Dark Age: 400 years without written records.

A Silent History

Imagine four hundred years without text messages or emails! A huge gap in our historical knowledge.

Oral Tradition

Silence on paper didn't mean silence in the streets. Bards memorized thousands of lines, giving birth to the Iliad and the Odyssey.

Bonus: The Code Breaker

For centuries, Linear B was a mystery. It wasn't until 1952 that an English architect named Michael Ventris cracked the code. He realized it wasn't a separate language, but an early form of Greek! This discovery rewrote the history books, proving Greek civilization was much older than previously thought.

Conclusion: A Phoenix from the Ashes

The Dark Age wasn't a total pause, but a time of rebuilding. By the 8th century BC, Greeks adopted the Phoenician alphabet, added vowels, and created a new writing system. This innovation ended the Dark Age and laid the groundwork for the rise of Classical Greece.
